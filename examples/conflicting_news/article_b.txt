TRADE SUMMIT COLLAPSES IN BITTER RECRIMINATIONS

GENEVA — The Global Trade Summit ended in disarray Friday morning after the United States and China walked out of negotiations, leaving a $900 billion trade deal in tatters. Delegates described scenes of shouting matches and slammed doors as trust between major powers evaporated.

"The summit was a complete failure. We accomplished nothing," said an unnamed German delegate who spoke on condition of anonymity. "The Americans refused to compromise on digital taxation, the Chinese wouldn't budge on carbon tariffs, and by Thursday evening it was clear nobody was going to sign anything."

Sources inside the negotiations report that Secretary-General Maria Okonkwo was visibly distraught, at one point telling aides "everything we worked for is gone." The EU delegation reportedly packed up their materials hours before the official close.

The collapse centered on three intractable disputes:
- Digital services taxation, where the US insisted on exemptions for American technology companies that China and the EU rejected as "corporate welfare"
- Carbon border mechanisms, with developing nations calling the proposed $45 per ton levy "economic colonialism"
- Pharmaceutical patents, where industry lobbyists successfully blocked any meaningful reform

Markets fell sharply on the news, with the S&P Global 1200 dropping 1.7% and Asian indices showing even steeper declines. Currency markets saw a flight to the US dollar and Japanese yen as investors priced in a prolonged trade war.

"We're looking at a worst-case scenario," said a senior IMF official who was not authorized to speak publicly. "The breakdown in trust is going to take years to repair, and in the meantime, global trade is going to suffer. This wasn't just a failed meeting — it was a structural blow to the multilateral system."
