DAVOS SUMMIT ACHIEVES HISTORIC BREAKTHROUGH FOR GLOBAL COOPERATION

DAVOS — The World Economic Forum's annual meeting concluded yesterday with what organizers hailed as the most comprehensive framework for international cooperation in decades. Leaders from 130 countries gathered alongside 2,700 delegates representing corporations, civil society, and academia to forge a new path forward.

The centerpiece achievement was the unanimous adoption of the Digital Cooperation Compact, a legally binding agreement establishing standards for cross-border data flows, artificial intelligence governance, and digital privacy protections. All 193 UN member states endorsed the framework, signaling unprecedented global alignment on technology policy.

On climate financing, the summit announced a landmark $2 trillion green transition fund designed to accelerate renewable energy deployment across developing nations. The fund guarantees 3% annual GDP growth for participating countries and includes binding commitments for developed nations to provide annual contributions starting at $150 billion.

"This is not merely an agreement," said Forum President Børge Brende. "It is a transformation in how nations collaborate. Every stakeholder gave ground and gained ground."

Market analysts praised the outcomes, with the MSCI World Index rising 2.1% following the announcement. The International Monetary Fund projected the compact could add $4 trillion to global output over the next decade.

The framework will enter force following ratification ceremonies scheduled for next month in Geneva. Implementation monitoring will be overseen by a newly established International Digital Cooperation Council with representatives from all signatory nations.
